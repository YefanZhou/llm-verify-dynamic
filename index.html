Variation in Verification: Understanding Verification Dynamics in Large Language Models

Yefan Zhou1,2, Austin Xu1, Yilun Zhou1, Janvijay Singh1,3, Jiang Gui2, Shafiq Joty1
1 Salesforce AI Research
2 Dartmouth College
3 University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign

Our study of LLM verification dynamics reveals how problem difficulty, generator capability, and verifier generation capability influence verification performance.

Abstract

Recent advances have shown that scaling test-time computation enables large language models (LLMs) to solve increasingly complex problems across diverse domains. One effective paradigm for test-time scaling (TTS) involves LLM generators producing multiple solution candidates, with LLM verifiers assessing the correctness of these candidates without reference answers. In this paper, we study generative verifiers, which perform verification by generating chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning followed by a binary verdict. We systematically analyze verification dynamics across three dimensions - problem difficulty, generator capability, and verifier generation capability - with empirical studies on 12 benchmarks across mathematical reasoning, knowledge, and natural language reasoning tasks using 14 open-source models (2B to 72B parameter range) and GPT-4o. Our experiments reveal three key findings about verification effectiveness: (1) Easy problems allow verifiers to more reliably certify correct responses; (2) Weak generators produce errors that are easier to detect than strong generators; (3) Verification ability is generally correlated with the verifier's own problem-solving capability, but this relationship varies with problem difficulty. These findings reveal opportunities to optimize basic verification strategies in TTS applications. First, given the same verifier, some weak generators can nearly match stronger ones in post-verification TTS performance (e.g., the Gemma2-9B to Gemma2-27B performance gap shrinks by 75%). Second, we identify cases where strong verifiers offer limited advantage over weak ones, as both fail to provide meaningful verification gains, suggesting that verifier scaling alone cannot overcome fundamental verification challenges.

🔍 RQ1: How does problem difficulty affect verification?

Finding: Problem difficulty primarily affects TPR of verification — easy problems allow verifiers to more reliably certify correct responses.

Figure 2. Each curve shows a verifier's performance across four difficulty quartiles. TPR (top row) rises steadily from hardest to easiest problems across all model families and domains, while TNR (bottom row) shows no consistent trend with difficulty.

Figure 3. Why? On the hardest problems, 49% of correct responses are falsely rejected (FN), and 18% of those FNs stem from the verifier making solving mistakes in its own CoT — confirming that faulty reference generation drives false negatives on difficult problems.

🔍 RQ2: How does generator capability affect verification?

Finding: Generator capability primarily affects TNR — errors made by weak generators are easier to detect than those made by strong generators.

Figure 4. Heatmaps of TPR (top) and TNR (bottom) for all 15 verifier × 15 generator pairs across three domains. TPR is uniformly high and changes little with generator strength; TNR drops sharply as generators become more capable (heatmap shifts from red to blue left-to-right in the bottom row).

Figure 5. Why? Stronger generators produce fewer surface-level errors (self-contradictions, basic arithmetic mistakes, incomplete responses). As these obvious errors disappear, incorrect solutions become harder to detect, causing TNR to fall.

🔍 RQ3: How does verifier capability affect verification?

Finding: Verifier generation capability correlates with verification performance in a difficulty-dependent, non-linear way — linear on medium problems, threshold-limited on hard, and saturated on easy.

Figure 6. (Top) Averaged over all problems, a strong linear correlation (r ≥ 0.90) exists between a verifier's generation capability and its balanced accuracy. (Bottom) Stratified by difficulty: medium problems (yellow) show near-perfect linear correlation (r > 0.90); hard problems (blue) plateau — extra verifier capability yields little gain; easy problems (pink) show a sharp threshold effect where even modest capability jumps produce large accuracy improvements.

⚡ Application to Test-Time Scaling (TTS)

Our findings reveal practical opportunities to optimize compute allocation in verifier-based TTS.

RQ4: Can a weak generator match a stronger one in TTS?

Yes. Given the same verifier, weak-to-medium generators can nearly close the gap with much stronger generators after verification, because their higher TNR leads to peak verification gains.

Figure 7. (a) Pass rate before (blue) and after (orange) verification with a fixed verifier (GPT-4o), across generators ordered by capability. Weak generators improve dramatically — e.g., Gemma2-9B approaches Gemma2-27B post-verification, closing 75.7% of the gap. (b) Verification gain (bars) peaks at weak-to-medium generators because they achieve high TNR (effective error filtering) while maintaining moderate TPR. As generators strengthen, TNR collapses and gains diminish.

RQ5: Can a weak verifier match the gains of a strong verifier in TTS?

In certain regimes, yes. The gap between a strong verifier (GPT-4o) and a weak verifier (Qwen2.5-7B) narrows on both extremes of problem difficulty and when using strong generators — but these are also the regimes where verification gains are minimal overall.

Figure 8. (a) Verification gain gap between strong (GPT-4o) and weak (Qwen2.5-7B) verifiers across problem difficulty and generator strength. The gap narrows at both difficulty extremes and for strong generators. (b) TPR increases for all verifiers as problems get easier, giving weak verifiers comparable correctness recognition. (c) TNR decreases as generator capability rises; the gap between verifiers also narrows — revealing that scaling from a 7B verifier to GPT-4o fails to overcome fundamental verification challenges in these regimes.
